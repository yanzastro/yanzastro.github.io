My research

In the last century, Einstein?fs theory of General Relativity enabled us to come up with a testable theory of the universe, and Hubble?fs observation presented the realization that the universe is expanding, and was much hotter and denser. The idea of an expanding universe, called the Big Bang theory, is supported by three observational results: Hubble?fs observed expansion, light element abundances which implies primodial nucleosynthesis, and the cosmic microwave background(CMB) left over from 380,000 years after the creation of the universe.

Diagram of the Universe?fs expansion

The fluctuations in the cosmic microwave background contain a lot of information on the history and composition of our universe. In particular, the rich detail about our early universe is included in the angular power spectra of the CMB fluctuations, which constrains the cosmological parameters in current models of the universe. The latest cosmological data strongly support an inflationary ?cCDM cosmology with a minimal six parameters to describe our universe. The next challenge in cosmology is to probe the physics of the inflationary period by looking for the signature of primordial gravitational waves in the polarized CMB.

(Left) Nine year WMAP image of the CMB temperature anisotropy and (Right) the CMB power spectrum from the WMAP (2012) in black, SPT (2011) in blue, and ACT (2011) in orange. WMAP data alone (shown in grey) is extended to the higher-resolution data (Hinshaw et al., 2012)

We intend to produce half-sky maps of total intensity and linear polarization at 10 GHz. This data would probe galactic synchrotron emission and also can help constrain the so-called anomalous emission. Therefore, the maps can be used with other surveys such as WMAP and Planck to subtract galactic foreground emissions and obtain more precise CMB data. In addition, the data will give us information about galactic emission components such as synchrotron, free-free, thermal dust and anomalous emission in the microwave range.
